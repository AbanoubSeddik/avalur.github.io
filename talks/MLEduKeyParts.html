Creating Machine Learning course from scratch

I have highlighted some of the key aspects that are most clear and understandable for us. The rapid development we see in many fields is also happening in machine learning. If you take a modern course in biology, for example, you'll see that 20-30% of the material changes each year because science and progress keep advancing. We need to provide students with the most up-to-date knowledge, which means frequently updating our courses. For sure this is also true for machine learning. Last year, I had the opportunity to develop a new machine learning course from scratch at NUP. I will talk about this and then discuss what is happening at the other universities.

When creating a machine learning course, we first look at what is available globally and how other universities teach their courses. One of the most popular courses is Andrew Ng's course on Coursera. Many modern machine learning educators and researchers have grown up on this course. Another course I reviewed is the Open Data Science community's course, which is available on GitHub. It includes well-crafted lecture notes, theory descriptions in PDF format, and practical assignments. Everything is available for free, and nowadays this is a common practice. Of course, there are many other resources available, like the book "Deep Learning" by Ian Goodfellow and Yoshua Bengio or open video courses with materials on YouTube. Such resources allow us to build a comprehensive course using a variety of materials. Moreover, we cannot only use the available materials I mentioned as a basis for our own course, but we can and even should use AI tools to improve our materials: for example, add practical tasks and tests to them.

What do you need to succeed?

  • Assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts
  • Thought out and resolved yourself assignments
  • Trust and openness, chat with students
  • Competitions like Kaggle
  • Better to record and share video-lectures
  • Interactive and emotional atmosphere, questions for the audience
To ensure success, the following steps are crucial: 1. Assemble a strong teaching team with both academic and industry experts to ensure students receive high-quality education and practical insights. 2. Crucial to have well-thought-out and resolved assignments. This is essential for mastering machine learning concepts. Students must practice coding and theoretical problems to reinforce their learning. 3. Establish trust and openness with students by engaging in regular conversations and addressing their concerns. 4. Create a competitive learning environment by incorporating contests like Kaggle competitions to motivate students. 5. Record and promptly upload lectures to accommodate different learning paces and ensure students who miss classes can catch up. 6. Foster an interactive and emotional learning atmosphere, even in online settings, by asking questions and encouraging participation.

How do they teach Machine Learning at Neapolis University Pafos?

Part 1: Fundamentals of Machine Learning

  • ML tasks types, examples, quality evaluation
  • Linear models, gradient descent, decision trees
  • Ensembles, random forest and gradient boosting
  • Neural networks and backpropagation
  • Building GPT-2 from scratch

Part 2: Advanced Machine Learning

  • Clustering and EM-algorithm
  • LLM: tokenization, interpretations, embeddings
  • Building AlphaZero from scratch
  • Generative and discriminative models: VAE, GANs, flows and diffusion
  • RAGs and multi agents LLMs
For example in our Machine Learning course at Neapolis University Pafos students write their code for algorithms like linear regression and decision trees to understand the inner workings deeply. The course also includes theoretical and practical tasks and culminates in an exam consisting of a 30-40 minute interview to assess the students' understanding. In conclusion, it's important to maintain an open and accessible teaching environment, leveraging modern tools and methods to engage students effectively. The materials for my course are available on GitHub under an open license, ensuring transparency and accessibility for anyone interested in machine learning education.

To address the question about course updates: Indeed, each year, the course needs to be revised by at least 30%. This is challenging for educators who recently developed the course and would prefer to reuse much of the content. While it might be possible to retain the same content in the second year, by the third year, significant changes are necessary. To manage this, our team regularly reviews and updates the course content. We assess which lectures need modification, which should be removed, and which new material should be added. It's a continuous process of integrating about 20-30% new material while phasing out outdated content. This can be painful, especially when removing well-prepared materials, but it's a necessary part of keeping the course current. The speed of development in machine learning doesn't seem to be slowing down, and it might continue at this pace indefinitely. Our course structure is limited to 13 lectures, so adding new material means removing some of the old. This limitation helps us maintain a manageable course size. Regarding the question about whether machine learning will ever have a classical, unchanging core, similar to traditional mathematics: Yes, I believe that eventually, there will be a core set of principles and methods in machine learning that will become standardized. These core elements will likely form about half of the course content, covering fundamental models and techniques like linear models, decision trees, gradient boosting, and neural networks. The remaining half of the course will continue to evolve with new advancements in the field. For deep learning specifically, it's becoming increasingly common to integrate it as part of the machine learning curriculum. The foundational concepts of neural networks are now essential knowledge, with practical applications across various fields like NLP, speech processing, and computer vision. This integration allows students to build on a solid base before specializing in more advanced topics. To address the issue of assessing new assignments and criteria at the beginning of a course: We set clear and fixed points for each assignment in advance. While the criteria for obtaining specific grades might be adjusted based on initial feedback and performance, the overall structure remains constant. Generally, achieving a passing grade is relatively straightforward, requiring about half of the total points. This ensures that students who complete the assignments diligently can pass, while higher grades require more comprehensive work. Competitions are a valuable tool for motivation but can demotivate those at the bottom of the leaderboard. To mitigate this, we make competitions optional and provide private feedback on individual standings. Public leaderboards are used cautiously to encourage a healthy competitive spirit without discouraging students. Thank you for your attention. I'm open to any further questions.
