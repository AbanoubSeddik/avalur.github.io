To make a long story short





Oral Tradition
(circa 100,000 BCE - 3,200 BCE)
Let's start with the brief history of human education and knowledge sharing development.
So THE FIRST STEP of our journey is oral tradition. It was the only way of sharing knowledge about three thousand Before Christ's Era: In the earliest days of human civilization, information was transferred orally from generation to generation. Storytelling, folklore, and hands-on experiences were the primary and the only methods for education.
So, oral speech is reliable!






Birth of Writing and Numeracy
(circa 3,200 BCE)
Let's go on!
Around five thousand years ago, in ancient Mesopotamía, the first known writing system, cuneiform, was developed. Probably, you have heard about it. This was closely tied with the invention of numbers and basic arithmetic, which allowed for record-keeping and administration of transactions, mostly in marketplaces.
First of all, there was needed convenient notation and the ability to accumulate knowledge. Fun thing to know about this piece of history is that the first forms of writing were about accounting and credits long precede the invention of money.
Well, clay tablets are reliable!
It is also interesting and quite well-known that cuneiform numerical notation was base sixty, unlike our base ten numbers, but they had a true place value system like we do, not like Roman numerals. So the position of each digit within a number determines its value.





Scrolls and Libraries
(circa 1200 BCE — 15th Century CE)
Ok, now we move on to the third stage, which was connected with Scrolls and Libraries.
The accumulation of knowledge led to the establishment of libraries, such as the famous Library of Alexandria. Scrolls made of papyrus or parchment were the primary means of storing and sharing information.
Well, papyrus is reliable!

The Printing Press
(1440 CE)
The fourth stage was the invention of the printing press by Gutenberg. It revolutionized education, as we see today. Books became more accessible and affordable for everyone, leading to an increase in literacy rates and the dissemination of knowledge.
So, books are truly reliable!

Verification problem




Malleus Maleficarum (Hexenhammer) — Hammer of Witches

Probably the best known unscientific treatise, which was very popular at the time..
The more accessible information becomes, the more verification we need.
This issue is as old as our history itself. HERE you see the Malleus Maleficarum, also known as the "Hammer of Witches". This book is a stark example of the lack of scientific validation methods that existed during its publication. Originated in 1487, the book was an unscientific guide used during the period of the witch trials in Europe which established the justice framework for witchcraft.
This book was accepted largely based on social beliefs rather than empirical evidence. Today, it is even more important not to fall prey to unfounded assertions. After all, we certainly don't want to fuel our decisions with a "Hammer of Witches".





Electricity and Telecommunication
(19th century)
With the advent of electricity, technologies like the telegraph and telephone emerged, allowing for real-time communication over long distances. This made the sharing of information quicker and more efficient.
The internet boom of the time was telegraph. It came into commercial use by 1840, submarine cables were laid by 1850. This allowed rapid communication between continents for the first time ever.

Digital Age and the Internet
(late 20th century)
Let's go on to the Digital Age and the Internet.
Here we are close to modern time: the second half of the 20th century saw the rise of computers and the Internet. Information became digitized, leading to the creation of search engines like Google, Yandex, Baidu, etc. This made accessing knowledge virtually instantaneous.
You know, the Internet is reliable!





Artificial Intelligence and ChatGPT
(21st century)
In the current era, AI technologies like ChatGPT have emerged, providing personalized and interactive learning experiences.
These technologies engage users in conversations, help in learning, and are trying to answer all your questions. All this takes the interaction with information to a new level. Here I want to emphasize that your native language almost is not important for the system anymore, because it simply understands and translates any language to the internal representation!
But is ChatGPT reliable? We will discuss it later.
The fact is that nowadays any information is extremely accessible! Even without an AI assistant, one has access to a massive amount of information.
For example, on YouTube, you can find an explanation for almost anything and to solve almost any problem, from repairing not working washing machine to executing the proper professional technique for a squat exercise, as we see here on the slide.
Moreover, often, you can not only watch the video, but interact with the information, change some features and learn by exploration. With modern tools, you can even create similar materials yourself.
For instance, using Universe Sandbox application, you can literally make planet Mars collide with planet Venus and see the result. This simulates modern scientific theories about the world and displays all the physics of the process on your screen within a few minutes. Moreover, I am sure there is already, or definitely soon will be, a similar application for any scientific area.
Ok, and we go on!
And the next huge thing is that modern AI assistants not only search for facts and information, but they also combine and even generate it successfully. We might even say that they generate a kind of new, original content. Partly, it is true even for these slides.
So we get an enormous amount of information, and the challenge is that we don't know at all whether it is true. Here we encounter a fundamental problem: is it possible to somehow verify the answers of assistant, that might be smarter than we are =) Or maybe we can at least align it with our goals and work together effectively? In fact this question is most relevant for education, how we can use modern technologies safely and for the benefit there?

The Influence of ChatGPT in Education

Chegg Inc — an NYSE-listed company focused on improving learning and learning outcomes for students
Now let's move on to the second part of the talk and let's discuss the influence of ChatGPT in education.
We start with the example of CHEGG company, which focused on improving learning and learning outcomes for students. This company, as many others, was hit hard by the launch of ChatGPT.
In May 2023, the company said, “In the FIRST PART of the year, we saw no noticeable impact from ChatGPT on our new account growth, and we were meeting expectations on new sign-ups.”
But students that paid Chegg to practice exams and get homework feedback turned to ChatGPT instead. As a result, the company share price decreased more than 40%. In August 2023 the company already said, “We’ve pivoted the company to harness AI to better serve learners.” Now they are building internal LLM in partnership with Scale AI.

Pros and cons of ChatGPT in studying

Useful cases

  • helps answer questions for tools with poor or unreadable documentation (for example, it can tell you how to start using GDB)
  • gives good and quick examples for some JSONs, XMLs with the necessary characteristics, or some fixtures to fill in the database
  • helps to overcome the fear of a “blank slate”


What it doesn't handle well

  • any task where you need to understand deeply the general context of the whole project
  • not typical problems in not so popular languages: if you ask to solve a dynamic programming problem in Haskell, it gives funny answers
  • for different mathematical facts a lot of the GPT-generated proofs are incorrect
So what are the pros and cons of using ChatGPT in studying?
Let's begin with the USEFUL CASES.
It HELPS answer questions for tools with poor or inaccessible documentation (for example, it can tell you how to start using GNU Debugger).
It PROVIDES nice and quick examples for some JSONs and other objects, or some values to fill in the database.
ONE of the main points: it helps to overcome the fear of a “blank slate.”
And WHAT it doesn't handle well:
Definitely it is ANY TASK where you have a large general context of the whole project.
ALSO, it is non-typical problems in not so popular areas: if you ask to solve a dynamic programming problem in Haskell, it gives funny answers, which are totally wrong.
CRUCIAL for us: for different mathematical facts a lot of the GPT-generated proofs are incorrect.

What are the reasons for not using AI assistant?



  1. Intentionally, so as not to accidentally violate copyright
  2. AI is not reliable yet. If you believe it, damage may occur in almost everything related to information security, protocol development, infrastructure, etc.
  3. C++ requires human verification =)
  4. ChatGPT has problems with generating correct mathematical proofs
You may ask why not using AI assistant, what is wrong with them? Let's DELVE into understanding what the issues are.
FIRSTLY, there is a deliberate attempt to avoid copyright violation. This is an important consideration in today’s digital age.
NEXT, we must acknowledge that AI technology has not yet reached a point of complete reliability. This is crucial because over reliance on AI without proper verification can lead to significant issues in information security, protocol development, infrastructure, and other related domains.
THIRDLY, when it comes to programming languages such as C++ or Rust, human oversight is still required.
LASTLY, there are specific challenges that ChatGPT faces, such as difficulties in dealing with mathematical proofs.

Let me switch gears and share my own workflow for teaching and lecture preparation.
FIRSTLY, I usually work in PyCharm complemented with the TeXiFy IDEA plugin. This combination brings together LaTeX, Python, and JavaScript all in one place. It allows using powerful autocompletion and inspections of PyCharm, and inverse search from pdf.
NEXT, let's discuss the AI Assistant. It is capable of assisting with translations in a broader sense. AI helper is not just restricted to translating languages; it can convert formats, elaborate short ideas into detailed descriptions, and even to oral speech, facilitate auto tests creation, and more.
THIRDLY, I'm using the GitHub Copilot plugin. It offers practical pieces of code, draft solutions, and generally helps speed up the writing of text content for my instructions or lectures.
LAST but not least, the Grazie Pro plugin checks for typos and polishes long sentences. Its purpose is to ensure your text is clean, concise, and clear before reaching your audience.
Here I'd like to show the concrete example from my own experience. It is producing the problem for the International Mathematical Competition.
First of all, I have chosen an appropriate problem from ancient times of my teaching at Novosibirsk State University. There I organized math contests with tricky olimpiad tasks. So I need to translate the problem from Russian to English and to make a Latex format from Microsoft Word.
You see, with the help of AI assistant; I can do it in a few seconds. Here I simply copy-paste the answer to the working area. Then I check the result and look at the Grazie suggestions. Here I don't accept it, because it suggests something strange about additional dot in the text after the word "elements", but often it helps to polish the text.
Then I compile Tex sources and look at PDF file.
Then I need to write a solution, so I think through the solution and type it in Latex format. So firstly, I write x^2 = 0, then latex Rightarrow command goes. Here you can see that GitHub Copilot helps me to write the solution very quickly; it even makes simple calculation with math formulas.
Then I write the second part of the solution. Here I need to prove that the product being equal to zero is commutative. So that ab=0 if and only if ba=0. You see, sometimes I disagree with the Copilot's suggestions, but often it helps. Eventually, I check everything, recompile Tex sources and get the final PDF file done. Really fast, effective and convenient workflow, at least for me. Maybe you will consider using something similar for your own practice.
